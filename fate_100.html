Fate RPG Sample Characters

A Complete Guide with Over 100 Ready-to-Play Characters

Understanding Fate Characters

The Fate RPG system is built around the concept that characters are defined by their Aspects - descriptive phrases that capture who they are, what they care about, and what makes them unique. Unlike traditional RPGs that focus heavily on numerical stats, Fate emphasizes narrative elements that drive both mechanical benefits and compelling storytelling.

graph TD A[High Concept] --> B[Character Core Identity] C[Trouble] --> D[Built-in Complications] E[Other Aspects] --> F[Relationships & Traits] B --> G[Mechanical Benefits] D --> H[Story Hooks] F --> I[Flexible Storytelling] G --> J[Invoke for Bonuses] H --> K[GM Compels] I --> L[Rich Roleplay]

The Five Core Aspects

Every Fate character has five aspects that define them:

  • High Concept: The elevator pitch of who your character is
  • Trouble: The complication that makes their life interesting
  • Three Additional Aspects: Relationships, backgrounds, beliefs, or personality traits

Real-World Analogy

Think of Fate aspects like the tags on a social media profile. Just as #photographer, #coffeeaddict, and #dogparent tell you something meaningful about a person and suggest what kind of content they might post, Fate aspects tell you what a character can do well, what motivates them, and what kinds of situations they'll excel in or struggle with.

Character Creation Process

Creating a Fate character is more like writing a character biography than filling out a stat sheet. The process encourages players to think about their character's story first, then translate that into mechanical elements.

flowchart LR A[Concept] --> B[High Concept Aspect] B --> C[First Adventure] C --> D[Trouble Aspect] D --> E[Other Adventures] E --> F[Remaining Aspects] F --> G[Skills & Stunts] G --> H[Final Character]

When to Use These Sample Characters

  • New Players: Jump right into gameplay without character creation overwhelm
  • One-Shots: Quick character selection for convention games or short adventures
  • Inspiration: Use as templates or starting points for custom characters
  • NPCs: Ready-made supporting characters with full mechanical stats
  • Emergency Backup: When a player's character dies or retires mid-campaign

Campaign Integration Tips

These characters work best when their existing relationships and backgrounds are woven into the campaign's central mysteries and conflicts. Consider how their troubles can become plot hooks and how their high concepts can drive the story forward.

Character Development and Advancement

All characters are designed at the "experienced" level typical for starting Fate campaigns. As they grow, consider these advancement directions:

Skill Advancement

Characters can improve their skills or gain new stunts based on their experiences. For example, a character who frequently engages in social situations might improve their Rapport or gain social-focused stunts.

Aspect Evolution

Aspects should change to reflect character growth. A "rookie cop" might become a "seasoned detective," or someone "haunted by past failures" might find "redemption through heroic sacrifice."

Stunt Development

New stunts should reflect the character's growing expertise or developing relationships. Consider stunts that:

  • Reflect specialized training or experience
  • Represent important relationships with other characters
  • Showcase unique approaches to problem-solving
  • Highlight signature moves or techniques

Adapting Characters Across Genres

Many of these characters can be adapted to different settings with minimal changes:

Modern to Historical

A "Corporate Hacker" becomes a "Telegraph Operator and Code Breaker" in a Victorian setting. The core concept of information gathering and technical expertise remains the same.

Urban to Fantasy

A "Street Artist Fighting Gentrification" could become a "Court Bard Challenging Noble Corruption" in a medieval fantasy setting.

Realistic to Supernatural

A "Detective Investigating Cold Cases" might discover those cases involve supernatural elements, gradually becoming a "Paranormal Investigator."

Storytelling Techniques for Character-Driven Narratives

The Aspect Web

Create connections between character aspects to build rich story webs. When one character's trouble intersects with another's high concept, you have natural story tension and collaboration opportunities.

Compel Chains

Use character troubles to create "compel chains" where one character's complication creates a situation that compels another character's aspects, creating a domino effect of dramatic situations.

Shared Histories

Even characters from different groups can have shared histories. Perhaps the "Reformed Art Thief" once stole a piece the "Art Historian" was trying to recover, creating an instant tension and history between characters from different teams.

Real-World Inspiration

These character archetypes draw from countless sources in literature, film, and television. Consider how characters like Sherlock Holmes (analytical detective), Ocean's Eleven (heist specialists), or The Avengers (superhero team) have been portrayed across different media for additional inspiration.

Practical Applications

Convention Gaming

These characters are perfect for convention one-shots where you need to get into action quickly. Print character sheets for a full team and let players choose roles that appeal to them.

Campaign Arcs

Use different character groups for different story arcs within a longer campaign. Players might start as the "Heist Crew" but later transition to the "Paranormal Investigators" as the story reveals supernatural elements.

Teaching New Players

These pre-generated characters help new players understand how Fate aspects work in practice. They can see how high concepts suggest capabilities, how troubles create interesting complications, and how other aspects provide roleplay hooks.

Inspiration for Original Characters
